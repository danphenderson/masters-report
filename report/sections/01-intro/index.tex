\section{Introduction and Review Methodology}
\label{sec:intro}

Blood-flow simulation sits between continuum mechanics, numerical analysis, and
biomedical interpretation. A stenosis is a useful organizing example because a
narrowing changes velocity, pressure, wall loading, and downstream flow in ways
that depend on the model tier and on the observable being compared. A
three-dimensional CFD calculation, a fluid--structure interaction model, a
distributed one-dimensional area--flow model, a lumped outlet network, and a
learned surrogate do not retain the same state variables or answer the same
question. The first task of a mathematical simulation report is therefore to
state the governing assumptions before interpreting the computed quantities.
Anatomical stenosis descriptors and lesion-imaging records do not by themselves
determine the functional flow quantity being modeled, so pressure-flow and
lesion-imaging studies distinguish anatomy from functional assessment
\cite[p.~4401]{DerimayEtAl2021CoronaryStenosisObstruction}
\cite[Abstract; Methods]{VanDenHoogenEtAl2022CoronaryLesionCTA}.

This report reviews the principal mathematical formulations used to simulate
blood flow and uses an idealized stenosis computation as a worked example. The
case study does not delimit the scope of the literature review. Instead, it
provides a concrete place to see how continuum assumptions, model reduction,
closure laws, boundary approximations, numerical verification, equilibrium
preservation, and observation operators enter a reproducible computation.

\subsection{Narrative Review Scope}
\label{subsec:narrative-review-scope}

The review is narrative and scoping in style rather than systematic. Sources
were identified from a curated bibliography, a source-provenance log, citation
trails within retained sources, and targeted searches used to check concrete
gaps in model hierarchy, closure uncertainty, verification/validation, learned
surrogates, and observation operators. The detailed search strings, source
counts, and triage categories are reproducibility metadata rather than part of
the main argument; Appendix~\ref{app:code-and-ai-use} records the review
provenance. The included sources span classical stenosis-loss studies, continuum
and cardiovascular modeling references, clinical pressure-flow observables,
benchmark and open-solver work, and compact learned-model context
\cite{YoungTsai1973SteadyStenosis,YoungTsai1973UnsteadyStenosis,GaldiEtAl2008HemodynamicalFlows,
QuarteroniFormaggia2004CardiovascularModeling,FormaggiaEtAl2003OneDimensionalBloodFlow,
ShiEtAl2011BloodFlowModelReview,BoileauEtAl2015Benchmark,BenemeritoEtAl2024OpenBF,
CanicEtAl2024Extended1DStenosis,ToninoEtAl2010FAMEAngioFunctional,
Blanco2018FFR1D3D,YuEtAl2026PINNHemodynamicsReview}.

The inclusion criterion is relevance to the mathematical reading of blood-flow
simulation: what equations are solved, which physics are retained or closed,
what data are required, what observables are produced, and what evidence can
support a comparison. Sources were retained only when they supported at least
one of those questions for the model hierarchy, closures, numerical evidence
standard, or 1D--3D observation operator. Exclusion categories were sources with
unresolved metadata, sources useful only for broad clinical burden or general
background, low-value surrogate-learning references that did not clarify a
hemodynamics-specific limitation, and sources whose claims did not affect the
worked stenosis example. Clinical pressure-ratio, CT-FFR, distensibility, and
wall-shear literature is used only where it clarifies model-dependent
observables. The review does not claim exhaustive coverage of all clinical or
computational hemodynamics.

\subsection{Research Problem and Questions}
\label{subsec:research-problem-questions}

The research problem is to connect the mathematical simulation literature with
a reproducible idealized stenosis example. The report asks three questions:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item What mathematical model hierarchies are used to simulate blood flow,
    and which physical assumptions and observables distinguish 3D CFD, FSI,
    2D or axisymmetric, 1D, 0D, multiscale, and learned formulations?
    \item How do rheology, wall mechanics, geometry, boundary conditions,
    numerical discretization, and output operators affect the interpretation
    and reliability of blood-flow simulations?
    \item How does an idealized stenosis case study illustrate the practical
    consequences of model reduction, numerical verification, equilibrium
    preservation, and 1D--3D comparison?
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Objectives and Contributions}
\label{subsec:objectives-contributions}

The report has two linked contributions. First, it synthesizes the modeling
and numerical choices that distinguish common blood-flow simulation tiers.
Second, it documents an idealized stenosis case study in enough detail to show
how those choices appear in a concrete computational record. The case study
uses the Julia package \path{StenosisHemodynamics}; its role is illustrative
and diagnostic within the review.

\subsection{Notation Guide}
\label{subsec:intro-notation-guide}

The main continuum symbols are used in their standard units unless a generated
numerical table states otherwise: $\rho$ denotes density, $u_z$ axial velocity,
$\eta$ dynamic viscosity, $\nu=\eta/\rho$ kinematic viscosity, $R$ radius,
$A$ cross-sectional area, and $Q$ volumetric flow. The case study uses the
physical variables $A_{\mathrm{phys}}$ and $Q_{\mathrm{phys}}$ when comparing
with continuum quantities, but the implemented finite-volume state stores
$a=R^2$ and $q=Q_{\mathrm{phys}}/\pi$. Thus
$A_{\mathrm{phys}}=\pi a$, $Q_{\mathrm{phys}}=\pi q$, and the section-mean
velocity is $\bar u=q/a$. Appendix~\ref{app:domain-specific-notation} collects
the full symbol and unit table used by the report.

\subsection{Report Organization}
\label{subsec:report-organization}

Section~\ref{sec:continuum-description} introduces the continuum description of
blood flow, from flow maps and balance laws to incompressible Navier--Stokes
and principal observables. Section~\ref{sec:model-hierarchy} compares model
families across 3D, FSI, reduced, lumped, multiscale, and learned approaches.
Section~\ref{sec:modeling-closures} reviews constitutive, geometric, wall,
boundary, and observable conventions. Section~\ref{sec:numerical-methods}
reviews discretization methods, verification, validation, convergence, and
observation operators. Section~\ref{sec:literature-synthesis} synthesizes open
challenges across these choices.

Section~\ref{sec:case-study} then presents the idealized stenosis case study:
model specification, numerical realization, verification evidence,
geometry-rest testing, and the diagnostic 1D--3D velocity comparison. The final
discussion and conclusion return from that example to the broader review
questions.
