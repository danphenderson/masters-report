\noindent\textbf{Abstract.}\par\smallskip
Hemodynamic results are often compared as though model dimension alone
determined credibility or accuracy. In practice, model tiers retain different
states, closures, data requirements, and observables, so a reported pressure,
flow, velocity, wall-shear, or FFR-style quantity depends on how that quantity is
defined and supported.

This report develops a mathematical reading guide for stenotic flow. It compares
resolved 3D CFD and FSI descriptions, lower-dimensional reductions, lumped and
multiscale models, and learned surrogate or operator maps by asking what state
is retained, what is closed, what data are required, what is observed, and what
evidence supports the interpretation.

The guide is then applied to an idealized stenosis case study. A reduced
one-dimensional model, finite-volume realization, manufactured-solution
verification, geometry-rest preservation, and a plane--tetrahedron 1D--3D
observation map form one controlled example. The manufactured-solution rows
show the expected implementation refinement behavior for the forced operator,
and the selected geometry-rest-balanced finite-volume method preserves the
declared rest family to roundoff. Under the reference-coordinate observation
map, the C23 (22.56\%) and 40\% cases give mean absolute section-velocity
discrepancies of 0.604 and 1.13 cm/s, with relative RMS discrepancies of 4.47\%
and 8.44\%, respectively; the largest reported cut-area discrepancy is 4.60\%.
These are diagnostic final-time discrepancies, not clinical validation errors,
pressure-ratio validation, or evidence of equivalence to a fully matched
transient FSI computation. The central conclusion is that model dimension is not
an accuracy ladder: each model tier changes the state space, closure burden,
native observables, and evidence required to support a hemodynamic claim.
