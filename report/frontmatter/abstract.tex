\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Abstract.}\par\smallskip
This report reviews mathematical and numerical blood-flow simulation methods
for continuum, large-vessel, stenotic, small-vessel, and low-shear regimes,
with particular attention to rheological assumptions and interpretable outputs.
The model hierarchy is organized by what each formulation retains, closes,
couples, and reconstructs, from resolved continuum descriptions to reduced,
lumped, multiscale, and surrogate descriptions. Across the review, closure
choices, observable definitions, geometry, boundary data, and numerical evidence
determine how computed pressure, flow, velocity, and shear-related quantities
should be interpreted.

A worked example illustrates this standard using a smooth idealized stenosis,
an $R_{\max}$-normalized Canic-derived one-dimensional area--flow model, and a
plane--tetrahedron quadrature observation operator applied to resolved velocity
fields for 23\% and 40\% stenosis severity. The numerical study is interpreted
as verification of manufactured solutions, assessment of rest-state behavior,
and a diagnostic comparison of one-dimensional and resolved three-dimensional
velocities under this specified observation operator: manufactured-solution
tests give observed $L^2$ rates near 1.70 for area and 1.50 for flow,
zero-inlet rest tests expose localized nonzero solver-coordinate flow that
decreases by about a factor of four from $N=400$ to $N=800$, and the matched
200-section comparison yields mean absolute velocity discrepancies of 0.505 and
0.966 cm/s, with RMS discrepancies of 0.664 and 1.87 cm/s, for the 23\% and
40\% cases, respectively. The case study therefore illustrates the review's
central argument: verification, rest-state behavior, observation operators, and
cross-model discrepancy bound the interpretation of computed hemodynamic
quantities.
