\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Abstract.}\par\smallskip
Hemodynamic results are often compared as though model dimension alone
determined credibility or accuracy. In practice, different blood-flow models
retain different states, impose different closures, require different data, and
expose different observables, so the meaning of a reported pressure, flow,
velocity, wall-shear, or FFR-style quantity depends on how that quantity is
defined and supported.

A mathematical reading guide for blood-flow simulation is developed using
stenotic flow as an organizing example. The review compares resolved 3D CFD and
FSI descriptions, lower-dimensional reductions, lumped and multiscale models,
and learned surrogate or operator maps through a common set of questions: what
state is retained, what is closed, what data are required, what quantity is
observed, and what evidence supports the interpretation.

That guide is then applied to an idealized stenosis case study. In that
setting, a reduced one-dimensional model, its finite-volume
realization, a manufactured-solution verification test, a geometry-rest
preservation test, and a plane--tetrahedron 1D--3D observation map are carried
through as one controlled example. The resulting contribution is
methodological: the case study supports implementation-verification evidence
for the forced operator, reports roundoff-level geometry-rest preservation for
the selected finite-volume comparison method, and reports final-time
section-mean velocity discrepancies under a specified cross-dimensional
observation rule. It
does not establish external clinical validation, pressure-ratio validation, or
equivalence to a fully matched transient FSI computation. The central conclusion
is that model dimension should not be read, by itself, as an accuracy ladder:
each model tier changes the state space, closure burden, native observables,
and evidence required to support a hemodynamic claim.
