\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Abstract.}\par\smallskip
This report reviews mathematical and numerical blood-flow simulation methods
for continuum, large-vessel, stenotic, small-vessel, and low-shear regimes,
with particular attention to rheological assumptions and interpretable outputs.
The model hierarchy is organized by what each formulation retains, closes,
couples, and reconstructs, from resolved continuum descriptions to reduced,
lumped, multiscale, and surrogate descriptions. Across the review, closure
choices, observable definitions, geometry, boundary data, and numerical evidence
determine how computed pressure, flow, velocity, and shear-related quantities
should be interpreted.

The worked example studies a smooth idealized stenosis using an
$R_{\max}$-normalized Canic-derived one-dimensional area--flow model and a
plane--tetrahedron section operator to compare two resolved velocity fields
labelled C23 and C40 by stenosis severity. Manufactured-solution tests over
$N=50,100,200,400$ cells produced observed $L^2$ rates near 1.70 for area and
1.50 for flow, with zero forcing-audit mismatch. Zero-inlet rest tests showed
localized artificial solver-flow drift that decreased from 0.03839 and
0.06339 cm$^3$/s on the $N=400$ grid to 0.00973 and 0.01605 cm$^3$/s on the
$N=800$ grid. At the matched final-time comparison over 200 sections, C23 and
C40 had mean absolute velocity discrepancies of 0.505 and 0.966 cm/s and RMS
discrepancies of 0.664 and 1.87 cm/s, with the largest differences localized
near the upstream shoulder.
