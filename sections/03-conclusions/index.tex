\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Implemented Model and Auditability}
\label{subsec:discussion-implemented-model-auditability}

The first research question asks what continuous model, coordinate map, closure
choices, boundary approximation, and discrete operator define the implemented
solver. The answer is the implementation contract in
Section~\ref{sec:methodology}. The reported solver is an
$R_{\max}$-normalized Canic-derived one-dimensional area-flow model in solver
coordinates $a=R^2$ and $q=Q_{\mathrm{phys}}/\pi$, with
$A_{\mathrm{phys}}=\pi a$, $Q_{\mathrm{phys}}=\pi q$, and mean velocity $q/a$.
The selected comparison realization uses the parabolic-profile baseline,
Newtonian rheology, the fixed-area characteristic boundary approximation, MUSCL
finite volumes, minmod-limited states, Rusanov flux, source splitting, and native
SSPRK3 time integration.

This specification is a contribution because it makes the numerical record
audit-ready. The wall evolution law uses the $R_{\max}$ normalization stated in
the implemented balance law, and the legacy pressure helper is not the evolution
wall law. The boundary invariant is an $\alpha=1$ fixed-area characteristic
approximation applied to the variable-$\alpha_{\mathrm{eff}}$ system under the
persisted subcritical-regime diagnostics. The principal C23/C40 comparison runs
use $N=400$ cells on $0\le z\le6$ cm, a timestep cap of $10^{-5}$ s, and a CFL
cap of 0.45. Other finite-volume, modal-DG, native time-stepper, and SciML
surfaces remain secondary implementation-health context rather than the method
that defines the main comparison.

The point of this contract is not to summarize every solver surface. It is to
state which model, closure, boundary approximation, and discrete operator produce
the reported verification and comparison artifacts. That restriction keeps the
later claims traceable to the actual generated records.

\subsection{What the Verification Supports}
\label{subsec:discussion-verification-evidence}

The second research question first asks whether the implementation converges on a
smooth manufactured solution. The answer is positive but bounded. The
manufactured-solution rows compare the implemented forced operator with a
declared smooth exact state, and they support bounded implementation-verification
evidence for that forced operator. The spatial rows show decreasing MMS norms in
the recorded refinement study, while the requested-timestep rows are read as
time-step insensitivity at the displayed spatial resolution rather than as a
clean temporal-order study.

The verification chapter also records boundary, CFL, positivity, conservation,
and secondary implementation-health diagnostics. These records are useful because
they make accepted runs inspectable: subcritical characteristic-speed signs,
realized CFL, positivity projection counts, mass defects, and backend or
sensitivity checks can be audited from the generated artifacts. They do not
enlarge the MMS result into a formal order theorem for every model variant or a
complete production-sensitivity record for the C23/C40 comparison.

\subsection{Consequences of the Rest-Equilibrium Failure}
\label{subsec:discussion-rest-equilibrium-failure}

The same research question also asks whether the implementation preserves the
geometry-rest equilibrium. It does not. The selected continuous balance law admits
$a=R_0^2$, $q=0$ under compatible zero-flow boundary data, but continuous
compatibility does not imply discrete preservation. The current MUSCL/Rusanov
realization exhibits non-well-balanced geometry-rest drift.

The size of that drift is decisive for the rest of the report. The production
comparison-flow scale is
$q_{\mathrm{comp}}\approx2.288/\pi=0.7283\,\mathrm{cm}^3/\mathrm{s}$ in solver
coordinates. On the finest rest-state grid, the zero-forcing $t=1$ s rest-flow
values are 0.7313 and 0.7324 cm$^3$/s in solver $q$ for C23 and C40, with
physical flows $\pi q=2.297$ and $2.301\,\mathrm{cm}^3/\mathrm{s}$. The peak
zero-forcing rest drift occurs earlier, at $t=0.01$ s, with 1.566 and
1.658 cm$^3$/s in solver $q$. Thus the artificial rest flow at $t=1$ s is the
same order as the production comparison-flow scale.

This is a material limitation, not a side diagnostic. The C23/C40 comparison runs
start from the same geometry-rest state and use the same principal operator.
Consequently, the later velocity comparison can be read only after the
non-well-balanced startup motion has been exposed. The next numerical change must
therefore be an equilibrium-preserving discretization or reconstruction, followed
by a repeated rest-state audit before any stronger production-comparison claim is
attempted.

\subsection{What the Diagnostic Comparison Shows}
\label{subsec:discussion-cross-model-discrepancies}

The third research question asks what velocity discrepancies are measured against
the available resolved 3D data under the declared plane--tetrahedron operator.
The answer is descriptive and operator-specific. The operator defines section
area, physical flow, and area-mean axial velocity on the available C23 and C40
resolved velocity fields; the 1D output is compared through
$Q_{1D}=\pi q$ and $\bar u_{1D}=q/a$.

For the 200 retained section targets, the signed mean velocity biases are
0.480 cm/s in C23 and 0.909 cm/s in C40. The mean absolute velocity discrepancies
are 0.505 and 0.966 cm/s, the RMS velocity discrepancies are 0.664 and
1.87 cm/s, and the relative RMS velocity discrepancies are 0.0277 and 0.0688.
The largest listed section discrepancies are 2.35 cm/s at $z=2.291$ cm for C23
and 9.92 cm/s at $z=2.261$ cm for C40. In the physical-flow observable, the
signed mean biases are 0.0472 and 0.0684 cm$^3$/s, the mean absolute
discrepancies are 0.0482 and 0.0709 cm$^3$/s, and the RMS discrepancies are
0.0509 and 0.0893 cm$^3$/s.

These numbers localize a two-case discrepancy pattern, especially the upstream
C40 shoulder, but they do not identify a mechanism. The flow/area decomposition
is bookkeeping for the displayed samples, not causal attribution. The radial
plots are retained only as qualitative secondary diagnostics; the radial-summary
numeric table is withheld from the main evidence path pending reconciliation of
the radial-summary recomputation conflict. No radial-bin numerical localization
claim is part of the principal result.

\subsection{Unmatched Conditions and Required Next Work}
\label{subsec:discussion-unmatched-modeling-effects}
\label{subsec:discussion-methodological-limitations}
\label{subsec:discussion-implications-future-work}

The unmatched conditions must remain in front of the interpretation. The 3D wall
model, wall motion, boundary histories, material parameters, prior transient
history, and current/deformed geometry status are not fully persisted in the
tracked comparison record. The 3D sample is at 0.9995 s while the 1D sample is at
1.0 s, leaving a reported 0.0005 s time offset. The extracted 3D physical flow
also varies axially by 7.7\% of the C23 mean section flow and 20.2\% of the C40
mean section flow. The available files do not determine whether that variation is
an operator effect, a geometry-state effect, a boundary/history effect, or a
property of the resolved run.

The required next work follows from those gates. First, the principal numerical
operator needs an equilibrium-preserving discretization and a repeated MMS and
geometry-rest verification record. Second, the comparison record needs matched 3D
metadata for wall, boundary, material, geometry state, displacement handling, and
sample time, or an explicit exact-time resampling protocol. Third, production
sensitivity needs to be recorded for the actual comparison outputs: grid,
timestep, startup, area-cut sensitivity, and operator choices. Only after those
steps would a broader output claim have the required evidence chain.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusions-limitations}

First, the implemented-model contribution is an authoritative specification of
the solver that produced the recorded artifacts. The report fixes the physical
and solver variables, the $R_{\max}$-normalized wall evolution law, the source-to-
implementation differences, the fixed-area characteristic boundary approximation,
the principal MUSCL/Rusanov/SSPRK3 realization, and the plane--tetrahedron
observation operator.

Second, the MMS evidence is positive but bounded. It supports the declared forced
operator and is reinforced by inspectable boundary, CFL, positivity, conservation,
and secondary implementation-health records. It does not establish a formal order
result for every variant or a complete production-sensitivity record.

Third, the decisive geometry-rest limitation is that the current principal
MUSCL/Rusanov realization is not well balanced for the stenotic geometry-rest
state. At $t=1$ s the finest-grid artificial rest flow is the same order as the
comparison-flow scale, and the peak earlier rest drift is larger. This limitation
has to be repaired before the C23/C40 comparison can support a stronger numerical
claim.

Fourth, the 1D--3D comparison result is descriptive. Under the declared operator,
the C23/C40 section-mean velocity record reports signed bias, mean absolute
velocity discrepancy, RMS velocity discrepancy, maximum discrepancy and location,
and relative RMS velocity discrepancy. The largest listed section discrepancy is
localized in the upstream C40 shoulder in this two-case pair. Radial plots remain
qualitative secondary diagnostics because the radial-summary numeric table is
withheld pending reconciliation.

The next required implementation step is an equilibrium-preserving
discretization, followed by matched 3D metadata and production sensitivity for the
actual comparison outputs. That sequence is the shortest path from the current
audited record to a cleaner future comparison.
