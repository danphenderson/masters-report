\section{Integrated Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Model Hierarchy and Question Matching}
\label{subsec:discussion-model-hierarchy-question-matching}

The first research question asked which model hierarchies are used for
blood-flow simulation and which assumptions distinguish them. The review shows
that the hierarchy is not a ranking from poor to good models. It is a set of
state choices. Three-dimensional CFD retains local velocity, pressure, stress,
and geometry. FSI adds wall motion and interface work. Axisymmetric and 2D
models keep selected resolved structure under stronger geometric assumptions.
Distributed 1D models keep axial area, flow, mean velocity, and pressure or
wall-law variables. Zero-dimensional networks keep pressure--flow relations
without local stenosis geometry. Multiscale models couple these tiers, and
learned models approximate maps whose meaning depends on the tier and data used
to train them.

The practical implication is that a simulation result should be read from the
question backward. If the question concerns local wall traction, a reduced
area--flow state is not the native object. If the question concerns wave
propagation through a vascular network, a fully resolved local geometry may be
unnecessary without appropriate boundary data. If the question concerns
cross-model agreement, an observation operator is part of the mathematical
claim. The idealized stenosis study demonstrates this point by treating the 1D
state and resolved 3D velocity field as different numerical objects that must be
mapped to common flow and velocity observables before comparison.

\subsection{Closure Choices and Interpretability}
\label{subsec:discussion-closures-interpretability}

The second research question asked how rheology, wall mechanics, geometry,
boundary conditions, numerical discretization, and output operators affect
interpretation. These choices are not secondary implementation details. They
define the problem being solved. A Newtonian stress law and a generalized
Newtonian stress law can produce different viscous and shear interpretations. A
fixed wall, reduced pressure--area law, and resolved FSI wall represent
different mechanical states. A Windkessel outlet, pressure outlet, traction
condition, and characteristic boundary rule impose different downstream
information. A section-mean velocity, wall shear stress, pressure ratio, and
flow rate are different observables.

The case study makes the closure contract explicit: the StenosisHemodynamics
example uses a smooth stenosis geometry, reduced area--flow variables, a
specified physical-to-solver coordinate map, a pressure--area wall law,
parabolic-profile closure, Newtonian viscosity for the principal comparison,
and a finite-volume realization. That list is not a software inventory; it is
the mathematical statement needed before any numerical result can be interpreted.

\subsection{Numerical Evidence and Observation Operators}
\label{subsec:discussion-numerical-evidence-observation}

The numerical review separates implementation verification, equilibrium
preservation, sensitivity, cross-model comparison, and validation. Manufactured
solutions check whether a declared operator reproduces a known smooth state.
Grid and timestep studies check resolution sensitivity. Well-balanced tests ask
whether a discrete method preserves a meaningful equilibrium. Benchmarks and
open solvers support reproducibility. External validation requires an accepted
target for the specific observable.

The worked stenosis example illustrates why these categories should not be
collapsed. The manufactured-solution record gives evidence about the declared
forced operator. The geometry-rest test asks a different question about
flux-source cancellation in a spatially varying vessel. The plane--tetrahedron
quadrature operator asks a third question: how a resolved 3D velocity field is
reduced to section area, physical flow, and area-mean velocity. Each check is
useful, but none replaces the others.

\subsection{What the Case Study Contributes}
\label{subsec:discussion-case-study-contribution}

The idealized stenosis computation contributes a worked example of review
principles rather than a universal blood-flow model. It shows how a smooth
geometry can isolate model-reduction and numerical-method questions; how solver
coordinates must be mapped back to physical area and flow; how boundary and wall
closures enter the model contract; how equilibrium preservation becomes a
discrete property; and how resolved and reduced models require a shared
observation map before their outputs can be compared.

The same example also shows what remains open. Stronger production comparison
claims would require a repaired or explicitly well-balanced discretization if
the rest-state test remains limiting, matched resolved-data metadata for wall,
boundary, material, geometry state, and time, and production sensitivity studies
for the reported comparison outputs. Those are not cosmetic additions. They are
the evidence needed to move from an auditable case study to a broader predictive
or validation claim.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusions-limitations}

This report reframes the idealized stenosis computation within the broader
mathematics of blood-flow simulation. The review connects continuum balance
laws, full-order CFD and FSI models, reduced and lumped hemodynamic models,
multiscale coupling, constitutive and wall closures, boundary conditions,
numerical methods, verification, validation, and observation operators.

The central conclusion is that model interpretation depends on matched
mathematical structure. A result is meaningful only relative to the equations,
closures, boundary data, discretization, evidence standard, and observable that
produced it. The source-to-source review reinforces this conclusion across
resolved CFD/FSI, reduced and lumped models, clinical pressure-flow
observables, open solver workflows, and learned surrogate maps: disagreement is
not interpretable until the retained variables, closures, numerical evidence,
and observation operator are stated together. The StenosisHemodynamics case
study makes this concrete for an
idealized stenosis: it specifies the reduced model, verifies the implemented
operator, tests an equilibrium property, and compares reduced and resolved
velocity information through a declared quadrature operator.

The numerical findings are correspondingly bounded. The manufactured-solution
record verifies the declared forced operator with zero forcing-audit mismatch in
the generated table and observed $L^2$ refinement rates of about 1.70 for the
area coordinate and 1.50 for the flow coordinate over the reported grid range.
The timestep rows are best read as timestep-insensitivity checks because the
CFL-controlled accepted timestep can override the requested timestep. The
geometry-rest audit is the principal numerical limitation: on the $N=400$
production grid, the final zero-inlet solver-coordinate rest defects
$\max_i |q_i|$ reach $0.03839$ and $0.06339\,\mathrm{cm}^3/\mathrm{s}$ for C23
and C40, corresponding to physical-flow scales $\pi q$ of approximately
$0.121$ and $0.199\,\mathrm{cm}^3/\mathrm{s}$. These solver-coordinate values
are about 5.3\% and 8.7\% of the comparison flow scale. Refining to $N=800$
reduces them to $0.00973$ and $0.01605\,\mathrm{cm}^3/\mathrm{s}$ in solver
coordinate, with observed final-flow refinement rates near 1.98 for both
severities. The rest-state finite-volume balances close near roundoff. The
production comparison diagnostics retain positive subcritical boundary margins,
zero positivity projections, and small finite-volume bookkeeping residuals at
the reported scale, but those admissibility checks do not remove the
non-well-balanced rest defect. In the diagnostic 1D--3D velocity comparison, the
mean absolute section-velocity discrepancies are $0.505\,\mathrm{cm}/\mathrm{s}$
for C23 and $0.966\,\mathrm{cm}/\mathrm{s}$ for C40, with relative RMS
discrepancies 0.0277 and 0.0688 under the declared plane--tetrahedron
observation operator. Because the resolved wall, boundary, material,
geometry-state, and transient-history records are not fully matched, these
quantities are descriptive cross-model discrepancies rather than validation
errors.

\paragraph*{Outlook.}
The next mathematical step is an explicitly well-balanced reconstruction or
path-conservative flux/source treatment for the geometry-rest state
\cite{PimentelGarciaEtAl2025HighOrderWellBalanced}. The next modeling study
should compare the current fixed-area characteristic approximation with
characteristic, impedance, and Windkessel-style terminal conditions under matched
diagnostics. The next inference step is parameter-identifiability and
surrogate screening for wall, profile, friction, and boundary parameters, using
spectral-surrogate and learned-operator ideas only after the model inputs,
training distribution, and validation observables are declared
\cite{Colebank2025HemodynamicsIdentifiability,CsalaEtAl2025PCNDE,VelikorodnyEtAl2025DeepOperatorStenosed}.
The next comparison step is a matched 3D record with persisted material,
boundary, wall-motion, and transient-history metadata, plus a repaired or
explicitly excluded radial-profile operator.

The broader lesson is methodological. Review-led blood-flow simulation should
not treat computation as a final figure alone. It should expose the model tier,
state variables, closures, numerical evidence, and observation map that make the
figure interpretable. The idealized stenosis example provides one auditable
instance of that standard and identifies the numerical and data-matching work
needed before stronger cross-model or physical claims can be made.
