\section{Conclusions and Limitations}
\label{sec:conclusions-limitations}

This report established a mathematical vocabulary for idealized stenotic blood
flow and used it to state bounded numerical diagnostics. The continuum portion
connected the flow map, material derivative, transport theorem, balance laws,
stress closure, Navier--Stokes equations, and one-dimensional area--flow
reduction. The model-hierarchy discussion then separated reduced pressure
ratios, reduced shear proxies, velocity diagnostics, and implementation
benchmarks from clinical measurements or resolved three-dimensional wall
quantities.

The numerical comparison demonstrated that the selected one-dimensional solver
can be placed in a declared diagnostic relationship with available resolved 3D
velocity data through section-mean and radial-profile output operators. For the
two included idealized cases at $T=1$, the 1D solution gives broadly similar
upstream and downstream velocity trends but overpredicts the near-throat
velocity, especially for the 40\% case. The largest errors are therefore not a
minor plotting discrepancy; they occur in the region where the reduced velocity
closure, boundary realization, and node-slab 3D post-processing operator are
least interchangeable.

The package benchmark suite records descriptor health, refinement,
native/SciML agreement, rheology and velocity-profile sensitivity,
OpenBF-style boundary handling, resolved-velocity checks, and Python CPU/MPS
comparisons for the emitted CSV records. It is an internal reproducibility and
implementation diagnostic suite; it is not clinical validation, full transient
FEM validation, or proof of Julia--Python scientific equivalence beyond the
reported metrics.

The numerical record does not establish 1D accuracy against resolved 3D flow,
does not supply clinical pressure evidence, and does not demonstrate
pressure-drop or pressure-ratio accuracy. The resolved-velocity comparison is a
preliminary diagnostic whose raw XDMF/HDF5 inputs are not archived in the report
package. The pressure-ratio and shear-proxy conventions are included to fix the
output vocabulary for later reduced-order studies, not as completed numerical
evidence in this report.

A stronger follow-up comparison would restore or archive the raw 3D inputs,
archive source hashes or release identifiers, preserve per-run diagnostics such
as time-step counts, CFL extrema, positivity checks, and residual summaries,
and then vary one declared closure or boundary choice at a time. A later
pressure-focused study would also need declared pressure-drop and
pressure-ratio outputs, plus an external comparison record if the clinical
motivation is to be evaluated rather than only framed.
