\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Coronary and arterial stenoses motivate a basic computational question: how do
geometry, wall properties, rheology, and boundary data affect blood flow through
a narrowed vessel? Anatomical narrowing alone does not determine functional
obstruction, so a useful computational report must distinguish the physical
quantity being predicted, the model tier that produces it, and the evidence
used to check the computation. This report stays on the mathematical and
numerical side of that problem. It studies a $C^\infty$ idealized stenosis
geometry, an extended one-dimensional area--flow realization, and a bounded
comparison with resolved 3D velocity data.

\subsection{Research Problem and Questions}
\label{subsec:research-problem-questions}

The research problem is to define and check an auditable reduced-order model
for an idealized stenotic vessel without overstating what the current evidence
can show. The report is organized around three answerable questions:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Which variables, wall law, rheology/profile closures, source terms,
    and boundary-state approximation define the closed stenosis-aware 1D
    balance law?
    \item Under the recorded finite-volume solver, time integrators, descriptor
    checks, and benchmark norms, what implementation properties are supported
    by the reproducibility and self-convergence evidence?
    \item Under the declared \texttt{CrossSectionQuadratureOperator}, what
    section-wise velocity and physical-flow discrepancies separate the 1D
    solution from the available resolved 3D velocity data?
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Objectives and Contributions}
\label{subsec:objectives-contributions}

The contribution is a consistent model-and-output specification for an
idealized stenotic vessel, an internally reproducible implementation and
benchmark suite for the selected extended 1D model, a targeted numerical audit
that exposes a geometry-rest preservation failure, and a bounded cross-model
velocity comparison against two resolved 3D computational datasets. The
comparison is diagnostic rather than clinical, experimental, or
production-accuracy validation.

\subsection{Scope and Limits of the Study}
\label{subsec:scope-limits}

The smooth geometry is a deliberate numerical device: one analytic radius
profile supplies controlled derivatives, wall normals, severity parameters, and
repeatable mesh families for the 1D, fixed-wall 3D Stokes, and later 3D/FSI
model tiers. The completed numerical record is correspondingly bounded:
pressure-ratio quantities are reduced model-output vocabulary, with no clinical
FFR or CT-FFR measurement relationship.
