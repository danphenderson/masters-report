\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Abstract.}\par\smallskip
This report specifies an extended one-dimensional model for blood flow through
an idealized stenotic vessel, records numerical-verification evidence for its
implementation, and compares its velocity outputs with resolved
three-dimensional data under an explicit cross-sectional operator. The physical
area and flow variables are mapped to the radius-squared coordinates used in a
MUSCL finite-volume discretization with SSPRK3 time integration. The
formulation documents the continuous source-balanced Canic--Koiter wall law,
the parabolic-profile baseline, variable-radius correction terms, boundary
approximations, and output conventions so that the model and implementation are
auditable. The numerical assessment is deliberately restricted to internally
generated diagnostics: sampled self-convergence tests, cross-integrator
discrepancies, descriptor and boundary diagnostics, stationary-Stokes
projection checks, and run-provenance records. At $t=1.0$ s, the resulting
section-averaged axial velocities are compared with two resolved
three-dimensional velocity datasets, with plane--tetrahedron quadrature used to
compute cross-sectional averages. For radius reductions of 23\% and 40\%,
respectively, the sectionwise area-mean velocity discrepancies have MAEs of
0.505 and 0.966 cm/s, RMSEs of 0.664 and 1.87 cm/s, and maximum absolute values
of 2.35 and 9.92 cm/s. The largest discrepancies are concentrated near the more
severe stenosis and in the reconstructed radial velocity profiles. Because the
wall models, boundary conditions, and transient histories are not fully matched,
these comparisons characterize differences between computational outputs; they
do not constitute physiological validation of the one-dimensional model or
support pressure-ratio or clinical claims.
