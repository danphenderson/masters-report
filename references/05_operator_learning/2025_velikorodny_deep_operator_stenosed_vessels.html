Abstract

Coronary artery disease in presence of lesions in the coronary arteries may restrict blood flow to the heart. Thus, a better understanding of the hemodynamics involved and development of rapid and accurate methodology will enable clinicians to assess patient-specific risk in order to administer timely interventions. In this study, it was proposed to develop and validate a new multifidelity machine learning framework to address limitations of the computational fluid dynamics approaches widely used to model hemodynamics in coronary and peripheral arteries. Proposed method significantly reduces the amount of experimental (including in-vivo) data required for training and will make the arterial pressure predictions less dependent on the availability of specific boundary conditions. Integration of various data sources allowed to introduce a set of physiology-related features (e.g., viscosity, blood flow, resistance, elasticity, etc.) and to achieve, in particular, an excellent agreement with mean values of in vitro pressure measurements: 1.2 ± 3.5 mmHg.

Introduction

Coronary artery disease can produce lesions in the coronary arteries that provide blood to the heart, such as stenosis (substantial narrowing of a blood vessel). As a result, blood flow to the heart may become restricted. A patient suffering from coronary artery disease can experience chest pain, referred to as chronic stable angina during physical exercise, stress or unstable angina when the patient is at rest. A more severe and acute manifestation of the disease may lead to myocardial infarction, or a heart attack. Anatomical data may be obtained noninvasively using coronary computed tomographic angiography (CCTA). However, CCTA cannot provide direct information on the functional significance of coronary lesions, e.g., how much lesions affect blood flow. In addition, since CCTA is purely a diagnostic test, it cannot be used to predict changes in the coronary blood flow, pressure, or myocardial perfusion under other physiological states, e.g., during the exercise, nor can it be used to predict outcome of interventions.

Patients may also require an invasive test, such as diagnostic cardiac catheterization, to visualize coronary artery lesions. Diagnostic cardiac catheterization may involve quantitative coronary angiography (QCA) to gather anatomical data on the coronary artery lesions by providing a doctor with information regarding the size and shape of the arteries. QCA, however, also does not provide data for assessing the functional significance of coronary lesions. Thus, QCA can under- or over-estimate the functional significance of coronary artery lesions. Therefore, some interventional cardiologists intend to insert a stent for every intermediate lesion found by eye-balling or QCA, regardless of whether the lesion is functionally significant. Whereas, other interventional cardiologist may incorrectly defer treatment of an intermediate coronary lesions which are flow limiting. As a result, eye-balling or QCA may lead to incorrect treatment decision for the patient, which may pose additional risks and result in unnecessary health care costs.

Fractional flow reserve (FFR) is the gold standard for diagnosing intermediate stenoses in patients with chronic coronary artery disease1. FFR is measured during invasive cardiac catheterization (coronary angiography) by insertion of a catheter with a pressure sensor followed by calculation of the ratio between the cardiac cycle averaged pressure values in distal and proximal sites adjacent to the stenosis during induced hyperemia. Therefore, the decision to treat a lesion based on the determined FFR can be only made after the initial cost and risk of a diagnostic cardiac catheterization have already been assessed. Moreover, despite the European Society of Cardiology recommendation to use FFR to guide revascularization in chronic CAD patients, FFR remains underused due to associated costs, its invasive nature, and the need for trained interventionalists2,3.

Most early attempts for non-invasive FFR prediction relied on solving the incompressible 3D Navier–Stokes equation in segmented coronary arteries4,5,6. This approach requires minimal assumptions about the physical behavior of blood flow, but carries high computational costs. However, clinical applicability requires fast and accurate prediction, which limits the applicability of full 3D models, the use of which is, furthermore, difficult to automate since they require additional pre/post-processing steps and have considerably higher computational cost compared to simpler models. Recent efforts to improve clinical applicability of non-invasive FFR prediction were focused on developing simpler and faster models based on reduced-order physics such as 1D blood flow or lumped parameter models7,8,9,10, as well as, purely data-driven approaches11,12,13,14,15. The latter method bypasses the need for generating computational meshes and prescribing initial and boundary conditions. However, it depends on the quality and representation of the data available for training. In addition, ML algorithms perform poorly, or fail to generalize when trained on insufficient data. In addition, predictions from purely data-driven approaches may violate physical principles as well as regulatory requirements. Researches are, thus, motivated to incorporate a priori knowledge into the learning process to improve generalization and constrain the problem16. Numerous attempts have been made to incorporate prior knowledge by adding physics-based computer simulations to the input dataset17,18,19,20,21. Another approach22, involves the use of automatic differentiation in neural networks to incorporate an additional term in the loss function, which penalizes any violations of governing physical laws provided by Partial Differential Equations (PDEs). This technique has found significant application in fluid mechanics23,24, which is particularly relevant to the current study.

In recent years, approaches related to operator learning, which involve learning mappings between function spaces, have gained significant popularity. One of the major contributions in this field is the Deep Operator Network (DeepONet) introduced in25. The original version of DeepONet has undergone several extensions, including the model POD-DeepONet26, Fourier-DeepONet27, and V-DeepONet28, among others. The architecture of DeepONet includes two subnets, branch and trunk networks. This structure conveniently divides the feature space with the branch network handling geometry-related features, while the trunk network processing all other features. DeepONet can be employed purely for data-driven optimization, but it can also integrate physics-informed principles. In this context, operators directly learn the mapping from any functional parametric dependence to the solution of PDEs28,29,30. It makes DeepONet particularly attractive for blood flow modeling in generic stenosed vessels. Given the complexity of predicting blood flow, it is prudent to utilize various data sources for training the model whenever feasible. These data sources may include results from in vitro experiments, CFD (Computational Fluid Dynamics) simulations, ML numerical solutions of PDEs, as well as in vivo data from real patients, encompassing demographic data, functional data, and imaging data as shown in Fig. 1. One suitable approach for working with multiple data sources is to consider it in the context of multifidelity methods. Multifidelity methods represent a significant advancement in computational science, especially for complex models that demand high computational resources31. These methods combine high-fidelity (accurate, but expensive) and low-fidelity (less accurate, but cheaper) data to enhance prediction accuracy. Multifidelity approach is particularly beneficial for optimization and uncertainty quantification applications, where it reduces the need for numerous high-fidelity model evaluations. Multifidelity models often use Gaussian process regression or nonlinear autoregressive schemes to correlate different fidelity levels. Neural networks can be considered as an alternative to probabilistic methods. In particular, DeepONet has recently gained popularity in addressing multifidelity problems32,33,34,35.

The investigation into the effectiveness of machine learning, deep learning, and artificial intelligence (AI) algorithms for non-invasive clinical estimation of FFR has seen significant progress over the past decade. Numerous studies have contributed to this field, exploring various aspects of algorithm development, implementation, and evaluation36. For example, an attention-based multi-fidelity machine learning model (AttMulFid) is proposed in ref. 37. The considered multifidelity approach is utilizing 1D incompressible Navier–Stokes equations as low-fidelity data and 3D ones as high-fidelity data. An autoencoder is employed to intelligently extract geometric features and a low-rank representation from coronary arteries. The uncertainty quantification is used to offer deeper insights into the FFR prediction. Nevertheless, no experimental in vivo and/or in vitro measurements have been thoroughly employed in this study. In15 the ML model was trained on a synthetically generated database containing 12,000 coronary anatomies, representing a comprehensive sampling of various coronary blockage morphologies. The target values for the training phase of the ML algorithm are computed using a reduced-order 1D computational blood flow model. Subsequently, a machine learning model is trained to learn the relationship between the anatomical features and the FFR value calculated using this CFD model. The ML algorithm is implemented using a fully connected neural network with four hidden layers. According to the described design this ML model cannot outperform the accuracy of the baseline CFD model, while it could be potentially used to speed up the reduced-order CFD calculations.

Recently, an artificial intelligence–guided quantitative coronary computed tomography angiography analysis (AI-QCT), trained using invasive FFR data, has been applied in several studies and gained significant attention from clinical community. CLARIFY38 presents a multi-site study involving 232 patients using an AI-aided end-to-end analysis tool using 3D U-Net and Visual Geometry Group (VGG) based networks. In39,40, AI-QCT is also used for post hoc analysis of two others extensive trials for detecting and grading coronary stenoses, according to their functional FFR risk. The AI-based evaluation demonstrates high diagnostic performance compared to the QCA reference standard and in vivo wire-based FFR data. It should be noted, however, that the FFR prediction by AI-QCT is based only on the QCA type geometrical data, poses no physical constraints on ML model and may potentially increase its accuracy only by adding new, albeit limited, invasive FFR datasets41. In addition, this type of AI models can only use a dichotomous FFR outcome, whereas a continuous outcome would be more appropriate, especially in clinically important borderline cases, diffuse coronary artery disease and intermediate lesions.

In an attempt to overcome the aforementioned constraints this paper presents for the first time a multifidelity machine learning methodology applied to blood flow modeling in stenosed vessels. The ultimate goal of the project and corresponding future articles is to integrate CFD equations into the specific AI algorithm in order to try to avoid the non-physical behavior of the solution, especially for the intermediate plaques, and to “guide” the training process, thus reducing the required size of the patients database for all sources of data, including in vivo sets (see Fig. 1). The hybrid approach, based on integration of CFD equations into a global AI framework, requires development and validation of CFD model optimized for this specific task. A requirement to be able to deploy the modeling package in clinics, as well as a currently inevitable need to generate a very large numerical database to train and validate a machine learning model argues against utilizing a high fidelity, fully coupled 3-D flow structure interaction (FSI) codes for the first demonstration of the AI framework. Therefore, a reduced-order numerical solver based on a simplified 1-D model, while retaining full FSI, capability has been chosen as the CFD component of the multifidelity modeling framework. It has the advantage of requiring only modest computational resources and it is easy to setup and maintain, while still being able to capture the main parameters of the medical interest, including the impact of realistic, non-rigid boundaries. The viability of this approach has been demonstrated in a number of previous works (e.g., ref. 7,8,9), showing that the reduced-order numerical models can still return adequately accurate for the FFR values.

Previously, the efforts for validation of the reduced-order numerical solver with the embedded semi-empirical stenosis model (described in the Supplementary information section), and evaluation of its parameters’ impact on the FFR prediction have been undertaken. The research involved two main tasks: the first was to obtain and fine tune numerical values of the resistance coefficients by reproducing experiments of Young and Tsai42 with flow parameters set within a physiologically-relevant range of clinical interest, in particular in borderline situations of intermediate lesions, being centered at a stenosis blockage ratio (BR) of 60% (measured by diameter). In order to cover the entire physiologically-relevant range and to include the region not studied in42, the experiments discussed in this article covered the BR of 50 – 80%. The second task was to assess the impact of the vessel’s elasticity, which was previously ignored by a number of researchers, both in the experimental and numerical works in 3D, e.g., ref. 4,5,6,10, even though it substantially alters the physics of the problem, and modulates the pressure and pressure wave velocity (PWV). The importance of accounting for elasticity is further confirmed by experiments, e.g., ref. 43,44, indicating that an impact of elasticity can be comparable with that of the viscosity.

In order to accomplish these tasks, a series of in vitro experiments has been carried out using simplified experimental setup (see Supplementary information section) designed for investigation of the stenosis impact with clinically relevant parameters and limiting an influence of the poorly-constrained and/or uncontrolled parameters, which inevitably affecting the flow field in the real cardiovascular system. The values of the resistance coefficients obtained in these experiments were incorporated into CFD solver and a series of numerical experiments has been carried out to validate the solver through comparing numerical results with experimental data. In addition, in order to fine tune adjustable model coefficients, the analysis of the results, despite the deliberately simplified design of experimental setup, had provided new insight on the interrelation between vessels’ elasticity and pressure drop in stenosis.

In summary, this study, albeit a reasonably simplified geometry, considers a comprehensive AI model, as well as a physically relevant parameter set, being essential for modeling of both simple and complex physiologically-relevant scenarios. The research highlights are summarized in the following key points:

  • An in house 1-D blood flow CFD model45 has been developed, and is briefly discussed in “Methods” Section. Several series of in vitro experiments46 have been designed and carried out for CFD validation, ML model training and adjustment of boundary conditions. The methodology and results are detailed in the Supplementary Information file.

  • A framework that seamlessly integrates disparate data sources into a cohesive predictive model is proposed. To the best of the authors knowledge, this research marks the first application of a deep operator learning approach to the problem of blood flow through the elastic arterial vessels with stenoses. Although, the most simple case of clinical relevance is considered in present study for purposes of demonstration, the framework itself will be pursued to allow more complex scenarios to be examined, including various 3D stenosis morphologies, bifurcations, multiple stenoses, gravity vector orientation, etc.

  • In present article the discussion and results are so far limited to in vitro experiments and CFD simulations. However, a universal methodology that can accommodate relevant in vivo medical data (as it is shown in Fig. 1), as well as integrated physics-driven PDEs solutions have been developed, clinically validated, and will be published in the follow-up studies.

Results

DeepONet and vessels modeling

In this study it is proposed to consider the stenotic vessel modeling as a deep operator learning problem. According to the notation detailed in the Methods section, a model considers outputs \(G\left(u\right)\left(y\right)\), where \(G\) is a nonlinear operator, a mapping from some space of functions into another one, \(u\) is an input function, and \(y\) is a point in the domain of \(G\left(u\right)\). In relation to present scenario, \(G\) comprises pressure and flow rate, \(u\) represents geometry-related features, and \(y\) encompasses all other features. Therefore, the output \(G\left(u\right)\left(y\right)\) can be interpreted as the target value for features at point \(y\) in geometry \(u\).

DeepONet operates with two separate subnetworks: “trunk” and “branch”, handling the inputs \(u\) and \(y\), respectively. This architecture has been developing rapidly in recent years and provides all necessary extensions to meet the needs of current research. Although the vanilla version of DeepONet implies a single operator \(G\), various strategies exist to work with multiple outputs. These were employed to model pressure and flow rate. The pressure output is subsequently post-processed, to obtain the fractional flow reserve—the primary focus of the present study. Multifidelity DeepONet is employed to incorporate both in vitro experiments (high-fidelity data) and computational fluid dynamics simulations (low-fidelity data) into a common framework shown in Fig. 2.

Numerical experiments

Numerical modeling experiments utilize CFD and in vitro datasets, with corresponding parameters detailed in Supplementary Tables 1 and 2 (see the Supplementary Information file). CFD governing equations and controlled in vitro experiments are described in detail in the Methods section. Governing partial differential equations are obtained from Navier–Stokes equations, specifically, from continuity and momentum equation along x-axis in the assumptions of pressure uniformity over the cross-section and a relatively flat cross-sectional velocity profile with a thin boundary layer. The latter can be estimated from a fluid viscosity and pulse period using Lighthill approximation47. This approach had been successfully used in a number of previous works, see, e.g., ref. 48. Similarly, the Fluid-Solid Interface (FSI) is described by relating elastic vessel’s cross-sectional area A to the pressure p. A purely elastic state equation is so far employed, which assumes that the Young modulus is strain independent, and does not take into account the visco-elastic effects. Following original experimental series (see Supplementary information file) a typical test section configuration for simulations consisted of a single elastic vessel with embedded rigid stenosis positioned at various locations as a function of segment length. The stenosed region was, thus, completely solid, while the host vessels maintained physiological compliance. This was an acceptable approximation of a most simple, yet real cardiovascular system considering that the plaque at the disease sites often hardens the vessel walls by calcification. Experimentally obtained boundary conditions were applied at the inflow of the proximal and the end of the distal vessels using upstream and downstream pressure and flow measurements. Windkessel parameters49, were computed using experimental data and compared to literature, e.g., ref. 9.

In the first numerical experiment, exclusively in vitro data is utilized for DeepONet with multiple outputs and referred to as DeepFFR. In the second experiment, the CFD dataset is used as low-fidelity data, while the in vitro dataset serves as high-fidelity data. For this scenario, the multifidelity DeepONet (DeepFFRMF) model architecture is selected. In addition, the residual learning and input augmentation approach leveraging low-fidelity (DeepFFRLF) and high-fidelity (DeepFFRHF) submodels are adopted.

The primary objective of both numerical experiments is to predict flow and pressure, as well as FFR calculated from the measured pressure values, obtained via the in vitro dataset. The latter was divided into training and test subsets, containing 201 and 68 samples (cases) respectively, which corresponds to 75% and 25% of the total. The architecture of each model is determined through hyperparameter optimization facilitated by the Optuna package50, utilizing the Tree-structured Parzen Estimator (TPE) sampling algorithm. Out of the 201 training cases, 13 cases (representing 5% of the total data) are used as a validation set for hyperparameter tuning. Training and hyperparameter tuning use MSE loss on pressure and flow predictions. For the fair subsequent comparison of models, similar hyperparameters for DeepFFR and DeepFFRMF are used.

Computational efficiency is a critically important factor in the field of fluid flow modeling. In our study, the training of the presented neural networks was conducted using a single GPU NVIDIA GeForce RTX 2060 SUPER. Training DeepFFR took 1.2 h, training the DeepFFRLF and DeepFFRHF −5.0 h and 1.1 h, respectively.

Performance evaluation for DeepFFR and DeepFFRMF

Based on the time-averaged pressure measured upstream and downstream of the vessel, one can calculate their ratio (FFR) and introduce the corresponding regression problem. The metrics used here are the same as those applied to pressure and flow measurements (see Table 1). In addition, the binary classification problem is considered, where FFR ≤ 0.8 is regarded as the criterion for the ischemia (positive) and FFR > 0.8 for one for its absence (negative). Ground truth FFR measurements include 27 positive cases and 41 samples without ischemia. The binary classification employs a ROC curve-associated metrics such as Area Under the Curve (AUC), specificity, sensitivity, precision, Negative Predictive Value (NPV), F1 score, and accuracy. Table 1 demonstrates these metrics, assessed on the testing subset.

Cross-validation is essential for assessing model generalizability and to avoid dataset-specific bias. Although the training procedure described earlier did not include a cross-validation, a five-fold cross-validation is applied to DeepFFR model in order to confirm that the fixed test set was not significantly different from the others and/or averaged one. The resulting performance metrics shown in Table 2 are in close agreement with those provided in Table 1.

The differences between in vitro (reference) FFR and computed DeepFFR and DeepFFRMF were: 0.0025 ± 0.021 and −0.0029 ± 0.017, respectively, with the same excellent agreement (r = 0.99; p < 0.0001). The differences between experimental and modeled data for instantaneous pressure variables were 1.1 ± 4.5 mmHg and 1.2 ± 3.5 mmHg for DeepFFR and DeepFFRMF, respectively. In addition, others statistics (see Table 1) were obtained, including RMSE, which was based on the same units as the normalized target values. Denormalized pressures RMSE were 690 Pa and 576 Pa for DeepFFR and DeepFFRMF, respectively, and denormalized flow rates RMSE were about 25 ml/min for both networks. Scatterplots (Fig. 3a, b) and Bland-Altman plots (Fig. 3c, d) illustrate comparison between in vitro FFR and the predicted FFR both for DeepFFR (Fig. 3a, c) and DeepFFRMF (Fig. 3b, d).

Of particular interest is the dependence of the FFR error on the degree of stenosis. The greatest discrepancy is brought in by cases with stenosis of 60–70%, which is consistent with complex physiological description of intermediated lesions (Fig. 3e, f).

Experimental validation and uncertainty quantification (UQ)

Figure 4 illustrates pressure curves obtained by the DeepFFRMF model for six testing subset cases (described in Table 3). For each case, both the ground truth and predicted pressures are shown, serving as an experimental validation of instantaneous proximal and distal vessel pressures. It is demonstrated in Fig. 4 that pressure predictions in the absence of stenosis (test 202) and in presence of the 70% stenosis both for the rigid (test 222) and elastic (test 236) segments, respectively, are relatively successful. Pressure drop in the absence of stenosis (due to viscous losses) is predicted to be negligible. The model correctly captures higher pressure drop and lower FFR values due to the greater compliance of the vessel in test 236. Tests 268 and 321 further demonstrate substantially accurate pressure values predictions for 50% and 60% stenosis of various lengths. Moreover, as can be seen from illustration of the test 410, the model can correctly handle the conicity of the vessel, increasing its outflow resistance, decreasing flow rate and leading to significantly higher FFR values for a 60% stenosis, if compared to others cases with a similar flow rate.

Each prediction used in the experimental validation is accompanied by a 95% confidence interval, which reflects the uncertainty in the model’s output. In order to compute these confidence intervals, the model employs the Monte-Carlo Dropout (MCD) technique51, which enables quantification of predictive uncertainty. During inference, dropout is applied to the network’s layers across multiple forward passes, introducing randomness into the predictions. The final prediction can be obtained by averaging the outputs, while ±1.96 standard deviation is used to calculate the 95% Confidence Interval (CI).

The quality of MCD uncertainty estimates is evaluated using the following metrics: Mean Prediction Interval Width (MPIW), Prediction Interval Coverage Probability (PICP), and the Negative Log-Likelihood (NLL). NLL measures the fit of the predicted probability distribution to the observed data, PICP assesses the proportion of true values within the calculated 95% confidence interval, and MPIW gives the average width of the interval. These metrics are also presented in Table 1 to illustrate that obtained uncertainty estimates are of considerably high quality. More details about the Monte-Carlo dropout are given in the original article51, however, it should be noted, that some criticisms of this method can be found in52.

Feature importance analysis

A post hoc analysis of the feature importance for DeepFFRMF is performed using the Alibi package53. The permutation importance method54,55 was employed in the analysis. The computation of feature importance is based on how much the model’s performance (measured by pressure MSE) degrades when the values within a feature column are permuted. The most important features (Fig. 5) are evaluated and it is concluded that non-geometric parameters, such as mean inflow rate, baseline pressure (P0), specific impedance, representing resistance of the system, are having significant importance among all. In addition, it can be seen that the density, viscosity and elasticity may also be substantially important when compared to other, in particular, less important geometrical features, which are not listed in this Fig. 5. It should be noted, that aforementioned parameters are often ignored in the AI-based algorithms employed for non-invasive FFR prediction.

Discussion

Both DeepFFR and DeepFFRMF exhibit outstanding performance, which may be in part attributed to the initial data quality and diversity of considered cases. DeepFFRMF model achieved excellent agreement with mean experimental pressure data (1.2 ± 3.5 mmHg) and FFR RMSE of the order of 0.017, which are similar to invasive accuracy. Sufficiently accurate results were also obtained for the flow rate, with the denormalized RMSE of about 25 ml/min. Despite these results, it is important to note that the primary goal of this work is not to present a final model, but to demonstrate critical physical and numerical capabilities of a specific methodology for future improvement and validation. DeepONet architecture was deliberately combined with a multifidelity approach to address the challenge of using heterogeneous data sources that vary in fidelity and acquisition cost. This approach is being extended to incorporate additional data types, such as in vivo patients’ measurements and integrated physics-driven PDEs solutions, treating in vivo data as high-fidelity and others as low-fidelity sources. The advantage of the multifidelity approach to be able to compensate for a limited amount of high-fidelity data will be further explored in future publications, which will also include clinical validation of the described methodology. The proposed architecture is modular and scalable, allowing new data sources to be integrated without retraining the entire model. The physics-informed variant (PI-DeepONet) of the DeepONet considered herein, allows for direct integration of partial differential equations into training. In other words, DeepONet provides a natural pathway toward a physics-informed formulation, without significant modifications to the core architecture.

Uncertainty quantification described above is considered advantageous to clinical specialists, especially for complex and/or preliminary decision making and in view of an insufficient accuracy of incoming clinical data. Monte-Carlo dropout (MCD) technique is popular for its simplicity and effectiveness. However, this technique is criticized in52, which demonstrates that the uncertainty estimate produced by MCD is not calibrated and that the choice of dropout rate can significantly impact the UQ results. Future research will explore alternative UQ methods, such as ensemble-based techniques, and comparing the outcomes. Recalibration issues can be resolved, for example, by using errors estimated from the experimental set-up, which, however, is beyond the scope of the current work.

Results shown in Figs. 3b and 4 demonstrate that the developed DeepFFRMF model can be considered as a strong candidate for further clinical application in view of a small amount of high-fidelity in vivo and in vitro training data. Moreover, it can be seen that not only magnitude of pressure (peak, average) is correctly resolved, but also the phasing of the pressure transients is captured well by the deep operator model, which has the important implications for clinical and hemodynamic descriptions, as discussed below.

It should be noted, however, that despite extensive previous research on the blood flow in elastic vessels, several important problems remain unresolved. Most notably, substantial number of both 3D computations and experimental efforts have neglected the effects of vessel’s elasticity. Moreover, previous experimental studies did not have sufficient spatiotemporal resolution to resolve the transient characteristics of the flow. For steady flows with both rigid and compliant walls, the pressure and flow are in phase, thus, many authors choose to neglect both elasticity and pulsating nature of the flow in their 3D CFD simulations5. In the case of the pulsatile flow, however, there exists a phase difference between the pressure (P) and flow rate (Q) can be expressed as an angle in P/Q phase space and depends (in the Windkessel terms) on compliance and pulsatility of the system as a function of inductance, capacitance and resistance of the flow49. Therefore, the accurate (or physically correct) modeling of the flow must take into account often neglected4 elastic properties of the blood vessels. Peripheral flows such as the ones in the carotid arteries are characterized by a relatively small phase angles, while in the coronary flow, the phase angle can reach relatively high values, leading to the out-of-phase behavior56. In the latter case, the peak flow occurs during diastole, when the myocardial muscle is relaxed and the pressure is low. While the time-average velocity profiles indicate little differences between peripheral and coronary flows, there are differences that arise in the wall shear stress, extent of recirculation lengths, and turbulent statistics, being, however, beyond the scope of the present paper.

Although, physics-based models with periodic boundary conditions should be considered in future to achieve precisely closed curves, the pressure-flow rate phase diagrams in Fig. 6 illustrate the advantage of the DeepFFRMF model with inclusion of a “low-fidelity” CFD data providing both more accurate FFR results and allowing for fair approximation of a phase angle as compared to the in-house CFD predictions. Moreover, similar to the experimental in vitro study43, in the presence of the coronary artery stenosis, a substantial decrease of the phase angle value can be, indeed, observed in Fig. 6, e.g., for the case 321 with a 50% stenosis, as opposed to the experiment 202 without a stenosis (in the P/Q phase space a phase angle can be shown to increase together with the area of a 2D curve). The phase shift further decreases for cases 268 and 410 with a 60% stenosis. Similar to the extreme case of a severe stenosis of about 85-90% reported in ref. 43, the pressure and the velocity were essentially in phase due to the dominance of the viscous and turbulent loss terms over the inertia of the fluid in the pressure drop equation described by Eq. (3) in the Methods section.

In fact, a better physical description of the phase shift in future is important from both hemodynamics and clinical points of view, due to its effect on the recirculation lengths, wall shear stress values, and the locations of transition to turbulence. The effect of the phase difference on these flow features can have important clinical implications, since larger recirculation lengths and regions with lower wall shear stress are responsible for pathological responses in endothelial cells and have been associated with higher incidence of atherosclerosis57. Moreover, parameter importance analysis in Fig. 5 has demonstrated that the future improvement in clinically relevant machine learning models of blood flow and pressure in the elastic arterial vessels with stenoses can not be limited to application of vessels’ geometric parameters alone, but should also include others hemodynamic and physiologically-relevant features.

A number of important limitations exists for this introductory scientific work. First, these are concerned with relatively idealized in vitro experimental campaigns, which had so far limited stenosis morphology, excluded tortuous vessels and simplified the data collection. In addition, to expanding parameter space by the sequential stenoses, impact of the stenosis’ shape, profile and degree of (a)symmetry, data collection should be improved, including additional measurement location to better characterize the change of the pulse form factor after passing through stenosis, as well as to establish more accurate synchronization of pressure and velocity measurements. One of the advanced velocity field and boundary imaging methods, such as Particle Image Velocimetry (DPIV), has to be considered in future studies. The improved accuracy and spatial resolution of this approach could be crucial for investigation of the effect of stenosis shapes and its physical interpretation. Second, some limitations of the simplified 1-D model can be distinguished via full 3D fluid-structure interaction simulation with visco-elastic effects, which will be also pursued to provide the full unsteady flow field for the analysis of the more complex physiological configurations, to study the phase shift effect between pressure and wall shear stress/flow and to fine tune 1-D models. Third, as far as ML methods are concerned, more accurate and detailed geometric representations should be employed for branch subnet inputs, potentially through the use of 3-D autoencoders or other high-capacity encoding techniques. To understand the impact of architectural complexity, current models should be benchmarked against established baselines, with particular attention to performance, accuracy, and computational demands. Future research will explore alternative approaches to uncertainty quantification, such as ensemble-based methods or Bayesian neural networks, and assess their performance in terms of predictive reliability and robustness.

In conclusion, the presented preliminary results based on a specific ML framework support the viability of adopting the multifidelity approach and establish this ML architecture as a strong candidate for further clinical application, in view of a limited amount of high-fidelity patients’ in vivo and in vitro training data available in this domain. Future studies will consider application of clinical data as a high-fidelity input and physics-informed learning, based on integration of new fluid–structure interaction models.

Methods

Governing equations and CFD simulations

The governing partial differential equations in non-dimensional form are written as follows45:

$$\begin{array}{c}\frac{\partial \hat{q}}{\partial \hat{x}}+\frac{\partial \hat{A}}{\partial \hat{t}}=0,\\\frac{\partial \hat{q}}{\partial \hat{t}}+\frac{\partial }{\partial \hat{x}}\left(\frac{{q}^{2}}{\hat{A}}\right)+\frac{\hat{A}}{\hat{\rho }}\frac{\partial \hat{p}}{\partial \hat{x}}=-\frac{4}{\hat{\delta}{Re}}\frac{\hat{q}}{\sqrt{\hat{A}}},\\\hat{p}-{\hat{p}}_{0}=\frac{4}{3}\frac{\hat{E}h}{{\hat{r}}_{0}}\left(1-\sqrt{\frac{{\hat{A}}_{0}}{\hat{A}}}\right)\end{array}$$
(1)

where

$$\hat{q}=\frac{q}{{q}_{max }},\hat{A}=\frac{A}{{A}_{max }},\hat{p}=\frac{p}{{p}_{max }},{\hat{A}}_{0}=\frac{{A}_{0}}{{A}_{max }},$$
$${x}_{* }=\frac{x}{\sqrt{\frac{{A}_{max }}{\pi }}},{t}_{* }=t\frac{{q}_{max }\sqrt{\pi }}{{A}_{max }^{\frac{3}{2}}},$$
$$\hat{x}=\frac{{x}_{* }-{\mu }_{{x}_{* }}}{{\sigma }_{{x}_{* }}},\hat{t}=\frac{{t}_{* }-{\mu }_{{t}^{* }}}{{\sigma }_{{t}_{* }}}$$
$$\hat{\delta }=\frac{\delta }{\sqrt{\frac{{A}_{max }}{\pi }}},{\hat{r}}_{0}=\frac{{r}_{0}}{\sqrt{\frac{{A}_{max }}{\pi }}},$$
$$\hat{\rho }=\frac{\rho {q}_{max }^{2}}{{p}_{max }{A}_{max }^{2}},{Re}=\frac{2\rho q\sqrt{\frac{{A}_{max }}{\pi }}}{{A}_{max }\mu },\hat{E}h=\frac{{Eh}}{{p}_{max }\sqrt{\frac{{A}_{max }}{\pi }}}.$$

In these equations \(q\) is the volume flow rate, \(p\) is the pressure, \(A\) is cross-sectional area, \({A}_{0}\) - cross-sectional area at equilibrium, \({p}_{0}\) is the reference pressure, \({v}\) is the Poisson ratio, \(E\) - Young’s modulus, and \(h\) is wall thickness, \(\delta\) - boundary layer thickness, \(r\) and \({r}_{0}\) are radii of the cross-section with areas \(A\) and \({A}_{0}\), respectively. \(x\) and \(t\) represent the spatial & temporal coordinates in each vessel, respectively. Variables under “hat” are non-dimensionalized and “max” variables represent expected peak values.

Equation (1) must be complemented by boundary conditions. For the purpose of this project, explicitly specified inflow profile q(t) is chosen as an inflow condition, and use 3-term Windkessel relation at the outflow boundary49:

$$\begin{array}{c}\hat{q}+\hat{C}\frac{{\hat{R}}_{1}{\hat{R}}_{2}}{{\hat{R}}_{1}+{\hat{R}}_{2}}\frac{\partial \hat{q}}{\partial \hat{t}}=\frac{\hat{p}}{{\hat{R}}_{1}+{\hat{R}}_{2}}+\hat{C}\frac{{\hat{R}}_{2}}{{\hat{R}}_{1}+{\hat{R}}_{2}}\frac{\partial \hat{p}}{\partial \hat{t}},\\ {\hat{R}}_{1}=\frac{{R}_{1}{q}_{max }}{{p}_{max }},{\hat{R}}_{2}=\frac{{R}_{2}{q}_{max }}{{p}_{max }},\hat{C}=\,\frac{{{Cp}}_{max }\sqrt{\pi }}{{A}_{max }^{3/2}}\end{array}$$
(2)

where \({R}_{1}\) and \({R}_{2}\) are far field resistances, and \(C\) is the capacitance for the outflow boundary.

Finally, to simulate the pressure drop ΔP across the stenosis, a 0-D semi-empirical model is introduced, based on the experimental correlation derived from the measurements obtained in the experimental set-up of Tsai and Young42. It was based on the viscous and turbulent loss coefficients, \({k}_{\nu }\) and \({k}_{t}\) and \({{Re}}_{{s}_{0}}\) the Reynolds number, based on the \({U}_{{s}_{0}}\) “free” flow velocity. This model can be expressed as follows:

$$\frac{\Delta P}{{\rm{\rho }}{{\rm{U}}}_{{s}_{0}}^{2}}=\frac{32\frac{({k}_{{\nu }_{1}}L+{k}_{{\nu }_{2}}{D}_{{s}_{1}})}{{D}_{{s}_{0}}}}{{{Re}}_{{s}_{0}}}+\frac{{k}_{t}}{2}{\left(\frac{{A}_{{s}_{0}}}{{A}_{{s}_{1}}}-1\right)}^{2}$$
(3)

where \({k}_{{\nu }_{1}}\) and \({k}_{{\nu }_{2}}\) are viscous loss coefficients, \({A}_{{s}_{0}}\) and \({A}_{{s}_{1}}\) are the areas and \({D}_{{s}_{0}}\) and \({D}_{{s}_{1}}\) – diameters upstream and inside the stenosis, respectively, and \(L\) is the length of the stenosis.

Numerical implementation of the Eq. (1), with boundary condition (2) utilized 2nd order accurate in space and time MacCormack two-step method, with integrated 0-D stenosis pressure drop relation (3).

By repeatedly running the described solver, the CFD dataset presented in Supplementary Table 1 was collected and consists of 5000 full cases.

In vitro experiments

Controlled experiments46 are a preferred supplementary scientific way compared to pure in-vivo data due to data availability, ability to focus on a specific phenomenon impact by keeping other variables fixed, and the ability to remove complexities of in vivo cases not related to the problem under consideration. The flow conditions used for the current experimental series were chosen to match a range of physiological conditions, i.e., rest, moderate exercise, exercise, by varying the Reynolds number (Re). In total, 500 experiments with various flow parameters and configurations were carried out. Mean FFR values were of 0.86 ± 0.15 and mean pressures of 60.2 ± 25.9 mmHg. In vitro data is processed and compiled into a dataset presented in Supplementary Table 2 with corresponding ranges of the flow and geometric parameters provided in Table 3 of the Supplementary Information file. A utilized subset consists of 269 cases, while the rest of experimental samples, which will be published in the follow-up articles, were not included, because they describe additional levels of complexity and do not fit the objectives of present article (see Introduction), including multiple stenoses, curvatures, varied gravity vector, as well as alternative stenosis configurations, all these in presence of other varied factors provided in Supplementary Table 3.

DeepONet and its extensions

The vanilla version of DeepONet is presented in25 and is based on the universal approximation theorem of operators. The universal approximation theorem is formulated as follows: suppose that \(\sigma\) is a continuous nonpolynomial function, \(X\) is a Banach Space, \({K}_{1}\subset X,\,{K}_{2}\subset {{\mathbb{R}}}^{d}\) are two compact sets in \(X\) and \({{\mathbb{R}}}^{d}\), respectively, \(V\) is a compact set in \({C(K}_{1})\), \(G\) is a nonlinear continuous operator, which maps \(V\) into \({C(K}_{2})\). Then for any \(\epsilon > 0\), there are positive integers \(n\), \(p\), \(m\), constants \({c}_{i}^{k}\), \({\xi }_{{ij}}^{k}\), \({\eta }_{i}^{k}\), \({\zeta }_{k}\) ∈ \({{\mathbb{R}}}^{d}\), \({w}_{k}\) ∈ \({{\mathbb{R}}}^{d}\), \({x}_{j}\) ∈ \({K}_{1}\), \(i=1,\ldots ,n,{k}=1,\ldots ,p,{j}=1,\ldots ,m\), such that

$$\left|G\left(u\right)\left(y\right)-\mathop{\sum }\limits_{k=1}^{p}\mathop{\sum }\limits_{i=1}^{n}{c}_{i}^{k}\sigma \left(\mathop{\sum }\limits_{j=1}^{m}{\xi }_{{ij}}^{k}u\left({x}_{j}\right)+{\eta }_{i}^{k}\right)\sigma \left({w}_{k}\cdot y+{\zeta }_{k}\right)\right| < \epsilon$$

holds for all \(u\in V\) and \(y\in {K}_{2}\).

According to the universal approximation theorem, neural networks have the ability to approximate any continuous function with a high level of accuracy, provided there are no constraints on the width and depth of the hidden layers. In addition, it states that a neural network comprising a single hidden layer can accurately approximate both nonlinear continuous functionals and operators. \(G\) is an operator taking an input function \(u\), and then \(G\left(u\right)\) is the corresponding output function. For any point \(y\) in the domain of \(G\left(u\right)\), the output \(G\left(u\right)\left(y\right)\) is a real number. In the network \({K}_{1}\) is represented by a finite set \(\left\{{x}_{1},{x}_{2},\ldots ,{x}_{m}\right\}\) of locations named “sensors”. They are used to employ discrete values of input functions. Hence, the network takes inputs composed of two parts: \(u\) and \(y\), and outputs \(G\left(u\right)\left(y\right)\).

To implement this approach, DeepONet includes two sub-network types: “trunk” and “branch”. They handle \({\left[u\left({x}_{1}\right),u\left({x}_{2}\right),\ldots ,u\left({x}_{m}\right)\right]}^{T}\) and \(y\) separately. The “trunk” network takes \(y\) as the input and outputs \({\left[{t}_{1},{t}_{2},\ldots ,{t}_{p}\right]}^{T}\in {{\mathbb{R}}}^{p}\). The “branch” networks (one or more) take \({\left[u\left({x}_{1}\right),u\left({x}_{2}\right),\ldots ,u\left({x}_{m}\right)\right]}^{T}\) as the input and outputs a scalar \({b}_{k}\) ∈ \({\mathbb{R}}\) for \(k=1,\ldots ,p\).

As previously noted, in this study \(u\) represents geometry-related features and \(y\) includes all other relevant features; accordingly, \(G\left(u\right)\left(y\right)\) can be interpreted as the output for the specific combination of non-geometric features \(y\) within the context of a given geometry \(u\). \(u(x)\) represents the degree of stenosis and is defined within the domain [0, 1], where 0 corresponds to 100% stenosis and 1 to its absence. Inside the stenosis region, the function \(u(x)\) decreases symmetrically to a minimum at the center and increases linearly toward the edges. Outside the stenosis, it equals 1. The following expression can be thus obtained:

$$u(x)=\left\{\begin{array}{c}\left(100-{s}_{ \% }+{s}_{ \% }\frac{\left|x-\left({l}_{{bs}}+\frac{{l}_{l}}{2}\right)\right|}{\frac{{l}_{l}}{2}}\right)* 0.01,{if}{l}_{{bs}}\le x\le {l}_{{bs}}+{l}_{l}\,{and}\,{l}_{l} > 0\\ 1,{{otherwise}}\,\end{array}\right.$$

where \(x\) is 1D space coordinate, \({s}_{ \% }\) is stenosis diameter (%), \({l}_{{bs}}\) and \({l}_{l}\) are length before stenosis and lesion length, respectively. Feature set \(y\) includes parameters such as blood density, blood viscosity, baseline pressure, and other relevant biomedical and physical properties. The complete list of features depends on the data source used to train the specific model. For high-fidelity models, the feature list is extended by incorporating outputs of low-fidelity models, following the input augmentation principle.

To get prediction \({G}_{\theta }\left(u\right)\left(y\right)\) of \(G\left(u\right)\left(y\right)\) it needs to merge outputs together and add a bias \({b}_{0}\in \,{\mathbb{R}}\):

$${G}_{\theta }\left(u\right)\left(y\right)=\mathop{\sum }\limits_{k=1}^{p}\left(\mathop{\underbrace{{b}_{k}\left(u\left({x}_{1}\right),u\left({x}_{2}\right),\ldots ,u\left({x}_{m}\right)\right)}}\limits_{\text{branch}}\mathop{\underbrace{{t}_{k}\left(y\right)}}\limits_{\text{trunk}}+{b}_{0}\right)$$
(4)

where \(\theta\) denotes network trainable parameters. These parameters \(\theta\) can be optimized by minimizing the following loss:

$$\begin{array}{c}{{\mathcal{L}}}_{{operator}}\left(\theta \right)=\frac{1}{{NP}}\mathop{\sum }\limits_{i=1}^{N}\mathop{\sum }\limits_{j=1}^{P}{\left|{G}_{\theta }({u}^{\left(i\right)})({y}_{j}^{\left(i\right)})-G({u}^{\left(i\right)})({y}_{j}^{\left(i\right)})\right|}^{2}\\ =\frac{1}{{NP}}\mathop{\sum }\limits_{i=1}^{N}\mathop{\sum }\limits_{j=1}^{P}{\left|\mathop{\sum }\limits_{k=1}^{p}{b}_{k}\left({u}^{\left(i\right)}\left({x}_{1}\right),\ldots ,{u}^{\left(i\right)}\left({x}_{m}\right)\right){t}_{k}({y}_{j}^{\left(i\right)})-G({u}^{\left(i\right)})({y}_{j}^{\left(i\right)})\right|}^{2}\end{array}$$

where \({{\{u}^{\left(i\right)}\}}_{i=1}^{N}\) denotes \(N\) separate input functions and for each \(\{{{y}_{j}^{\left(i\right)}\}}_{j=1}^{P}\) are \(P\) locations in the domain of \(G({u}^{\left(i\right)})\), and \(G({u}^{\left(i\right)})({y}_{j}^{\left(i\right)})\) is the corresponding output data evaluated at \({y}_{j}^{\left(i\right)}\).

Using lots of branch networks is computationally and memory expensive and all the “branch” networks might be merged into one single “branch” network. This implementation is named “unstacked DeepONet”, as opposed to the implementation with multiple independent “branch” networks named “stacked DeepONet”.

As could be seen, the output of a vanilla DeepONet is a scalar, while the required solution might be a 2-dimensional vector. To resolve this issue, the forward pass is modified such that the DeepONet output can be a vector. In29 authors suppose that a DeepONet outputs n different scalar functions and (4) is modified as following:

$${G}_{\theta }^{\left(i\right)}\left(u\right)\left(y\right)=\mathop{\sum }\limits_{k={p}_{i-1}+1}^{{p}_{i}}\left(\mathop{\underbrace{{b}_{k}\left(u\left({x}_{1}\right),u\left({x}_{2}\right),\ldots ,u\left({x}_{m}\right)\right)}}\limits_{\text{branch}}\mathop{\underbrace{{t}_{k}\left(y\right)}}\limits_{\text{trunk}}+{b}_{0}^{\left(i\right)}\right)$$

for \(i=1,\ldots ,n\) where \(0={p}_{0} < {p}_{1} < \ldots < {p}_{n}=p\). The multiple outputs issue is also discussed in26. The following strategies were proposed without any particular comparison:

  1. 1.

    Independent strategy. To use n independent DeepONets, each DeepONet outputs only one function.

  2. 2.

    “Split both” strategy. To split the outputs of both the “branch” net and the “trunk” net into n groups, and then the kth group outputs the kth solution.

  3. 3.

    “Split branch” strategy. To split the “branch” net and share the “trunk” net.

  4. 4.

    “Split trunk” strategy. To split the “trunk” net and share the “branch” net.

In34 methods of residual learning and input augmentation are combined for multifidelity DeepONet training. Low-fidelity model is trained as described earlier and high-fidelity model learns to predict the residual between the high-fidelity and low-fidelity outputs:

$${\mathscr{R}}{\mathscr{(}}u)(y)={G}_{{HF}}(u)(y)-{G}_{{LF}}(u)(y).$$
(5)

It is assumed that \({G}_{{HF}}\) and \({G}_{{LF}}\) exhibit similar behaviors and that should make their difference \({\mathscr{R}}\) easy to learn. In the framework of the input augmentation, the low-fidelity prediction is used as an additional input of the trunk net of high-fidelity DeepONet:

$${G}_{HF}(u)(y)=Q(u)(y,{G}_{LF}(u)(y))$$
(6)

where \({\mathscr{Q}}\) is the operator learned by high-fidelity DeepONet34. Also describes an alternative approach to input augmentation when low-fidelity prediction \({G}_{{LF}}\left(u\right)\) is appended to the branch net of high-fidelity DeepONet:

$${G}_{HF}(u)(y)=Q(u,{G}_{LF}(u))(y)$$

however, for further simplicity only the first approach is considered. From (5, 6) the final prediction of the high-fidelity solution is defined as:

$${G}_{{HF}}(u)(y)=\mathop{\underbrace{{G}_{{LF}}(u)(y)}}\limits_{{\rm{LF\; DeepONet}}}+\mathop{\underbrace{{\mathscr{R}}{\mathscr{(}}u)(y,\mathop{\overbrace{{G}_{{LF}}(u)(y)}}\limits^{\text{LF}{\rm{DeepONet}}})}}\limits_{{\rm{HF\; DeepONet}}}.$$
